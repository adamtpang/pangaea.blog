Speed gets treated as a personality trait. Some people are fast, some are careful, and we file it under temperament. That is a dodge. Every schedule is a decision about who waits.

A cure delayed a decade is a decade of funerals. A tool shipped next year is a year of people doing it the hard way. The cost of slowness is real, it is just paid by strangers you will never meet, which is exactly what makes it easy to ignore. Caution feels virtuous because its victims are invisible.

I am not arguing for recklessness. Reckless is fast in a direction you have not checked. The sequence runs the other way: courage, then experiment, then knowledge, then clarity, and only then speed. Speed is the last thing that shows up, not the first. When you actually understand the problem, moving slowly stops feeling responsible and starts feeling like a choice you are making with someone else's time.

Most of what looks like diligence is fear wearing a good outfit. Another round of research, another draft nobody reads, another meeting to align. Delete the step and ask what actually broke. Usually nothing did.

This is also why building in public compounds. Your finished thing helps whoever uses it. Your process helps everyone attempting the same thing behind you, which multiplies the effect of finishing at all.

So treat the pace of your work as an ethical question and not an aesthetic one. Ask who is waiting, and whether they agreed to.

There is no speed limit on the seeds you can plant.
