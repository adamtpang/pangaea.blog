A company is a fiction. So is money. We agree to believe in them, and the belief is wildly useful, but it is still a story we tell each other with a straight face.

This is not cynicism. It is clarifying. Underneath the stories there is a real economy, and it is made of only a few things: useful goods, useful services, and the assets that produce them. Everything else is scorekeeping.

The truest measure I know is time. How many man-hours does it take to produce one real thing: a loaf, a house, a working piece of software, an hour of someone feeling less alone. Drive that number down and you have actually made the world richer. Move money around without touching that number and you have only rearranged the fiction.

The people who seem to bend the world tend to see this. They are not chasing the score. They are obsessed with the thing underneath it: more real output per human hour, cheaper and better, for more people. The money follows, because money is a claim on that output, never the output itself.

It helps to hold both at once. Take the fiction seriously enough to use it, because it is how millions of strangers coordinate. But never mistake it for the real economy underneath, the goods and services and hours that are the only things that were ever actually there.

So make useful things for each other. The rest is a story about who gets what, and honestly, it is kind of funny once you see it.
