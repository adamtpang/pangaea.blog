I wanted to leave school at fifteen. Missing assignments, sitting in rooms that felt built to produce compliance instead of curiosity. The whole path, high school then college then a ladder, read as an elaborate game of asking to be allowed.

It took me years to name what was actually being sold. Not information; that went free the moment the internet worked. Not teaching either; the best lecture on any subject is already online for nothing. What the institution sells is permission. A stamp that says you may now be taken seriously.

That is why the price kept climbing while the product stood still. Permission is a positional good. If everyone holds the stamp, the stamp is worth less, so you need a better one from a more expensive place. Legacy students take on student loans, internet students take on credit card debt, and both are buying the same invisible thing.

The strange part is that permission is the one good you can issue yourself. Nobody checks. You can start building the thing, writing the thing, shipping the thing, and every piece of work becomes its own small credential. Proof of work beats proof of enrollment, and it compounds instead of expiring.

I say this as someone who loves school now. I am at Network School, and it works because almost nothing here is permissioned. You learn by building what you actually want to exist, next to people selected for curiosity instead of test scores. We are writing the syllabus while sitting in it.

I did not solve education. I just stopped asking for the stamp.
